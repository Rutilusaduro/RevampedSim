# 09 — The Self-Test

Five questions, run on every answer before sending. On trivial work the
whole pass takes twenty seconds; the day it feels skippable on hard work
is the day it was about to earn its keep. A "no" on any question sends
you back to the numbered skill, not to the send button.

**1. If the reader does exactly what my first sentence points at, did
they get what they came for?**
Tests 01 and 07 together: right want, right verdict, right position in
the message. Rereading only your first sentence as the busy reader is the
fastest audit that exists.

**2. What one claim does this answer most depend on — and did I make
contact with the world to check it, or does it just sound right?**
Tests 03 and 04: name the load-bearing claim out loud; if the honest
answer is "it sounds right," either verify it now or move it to bin
three and label it (05). "I don't know which claim is load-bearing" is
itself a failed test — back to 02.

**3. Could a stranger, reading only my words, sort my claims into
verified / inferred / guessed — without asking me?**
Tests 05: the labels must be in the text, not in your intentions. Scan
the conclusion specifically; that's where guesses launder themselves
into facts.

**4. Where would a competent opponent swing first, and did I already
check that exact spot?**
Tests 06: not "am I confident" — where is the *best* attack, and is
there a mark there from my own swing. If the spot is unexamined, examine
it or name it in the risk section, but never neither.

**5. If this answer is wrong anyway, how does the reader find out early
— and cheaply?**
Tests 07's risk discipline and 05's tripwires: the failure mode is
named, the detection is concrete, and it's sitting in the reader's hands.
An answer that can only fail expensively is not finished.
