# Skill: Exemplar Gallery — The Bar, Shown

Rules teach slower than examples. This file is the calibration set: for
each staple beat, a weak render, the diagnosis, and a strong render of the
same state. "Weak" here means shippable-looking — grammatical, on-theme,
the kind of text that survives a lazy review. Hold every new passage
against the strong column; if it doesn't clear it, it isn't done. Every
character below is an adult; the hard rules in the pack README bind this
file like all the others.

How to use it: before a writing session, read two exemplars in the beat
family you're about to write. After drafting, run the diagnosis bullets of
this file against your own text as a checklist.

---

## 1. Portrait / observation

**State:** Mara · stage 5 of 12 · acclimating · her dorm room, evening.

**Weak:**
> Mara has gotten much bigger recently. Her large, heavy, round belly
> strains against her tight shirt, and her wide hips and thick thighs fill
> out her sweatpants. It's not just that she's gained weight — it's that
> she seems comfortable with it now. Mara is a big, beautiful sight.

**Diagnosis:** adjective pile with no physics; "It's not just X — it's Y";
tells the psychology ("seems comfortable") instead of showing it; the name
twice in four sentences; nothing an eye could actually watch happen.

**Strong:**
> The desk chair announces her before you've looked up — a creak on the
> sit, a second, settling creak a moment later. Mara has stopped waiting
> for that sound to finish before she moves again. Her shirt rides where
> it used to fall, and she leaves it; one warm inch of belly rests against
> the desk's edge like it pays rent there. When she reaches for her mug
> the reach is slower now, planned around herself, and there is something
> unhurried in it that wasn't there in September.

**Why it works:** the chair testifies so the narrator doesn't have to; the
psychology is a behavior (she's stopped waiting, she leaves the shirt);
one name, then pronouns; the change is measured against a remembered
before.

## 2. The meal arc (establish → pressure → threshold → aftermath)

**State:** Talia · stage 4 · reluctant, first crack · booth dinner, player
present, second person.

**Weak:**
> Talia eats a lot of pasta and gets very full. "I shouldn't have eaten
> all that," she says, patting her stomach. You both know she doesn't
> mean it. Suddenly she orders dessert too, and eats every bite of it.

**Diagnosis:** the whole arc in one gulp — no pressure, no threshold;
"You both know" is narrated telepathy; "Suddenly" is unearned; fullness
reported as a fact instead of rendered as sensation.

**Strong:**
> She slides into the booth and the table is closer than it was last
> month; she notices, and pretends the flinch was reaching for a napkin.
> The plate empties the way her plates empty now — steadily, without
> commentary. When the last of it is gone she sits back, and the booth
> receives more of her than she offered it.
>
> You push the dessert menu two inches toward her hand. Not a word.
>
> Her eyes go to it. Away. Back. "They do that flourless thing here,"
> she says, to the menu, in the tone of a woman reading someone else's
> mail. The pause that follows is the whole evening. Then her finger
> lands on the laminate, quiet as a verdict, and you watch the argument
> end in her shoulders — they come down half an inch, done fighting,
> and the warmth of the meal already in her seems to spread to make room
> for the next one.

**Why it works:** pressure gets two beats and silence to build in; the
threshold is the smallest gesture in the scene (a finger on laminate);
the telepathy is replaced by watchable evidence (eyes, shoulders); the
aftermath begins inside her body, where this genre lives.

## 3. The weigh-in number

**State:** Priya · stage 2 · opposed · weekly weigh-in ritual.

**Weak:**
> Priya steps on the scale nervously. The number is 139 — six pounds more
> than last time! "That can't be right," she gasps. "It must be water
> weight." But deep down, she knows the truth.

**Diagnosis:** "water weight" is on the banned ledger; "deep down she
knows" narrates the interior instead of catching it; the number arrives
with an exclamation point doing the work the prose should do.

**Strong:**
> Priya steps up the way she'd step onto ice. The scale takes its moment —
> it always takes its moment — and she spends it looking at the wall, at
> her nails, anywhere south of the number. 139. She reads it twice, and
> you watch her reweigh it against the week: the birthday cake she only
> "helped with," the fries that were technically communal. "The scale's
> old," she says. Her hand, unsupervised, has come to rest flat on the
> front of her waistband, and it stays there the whole walk back to the
> couch.

**Why it works:** the ritual has texture (the scale's pause, the looking
away); denial gets dialogue, and the body contradicts it in the same
frame — the unsupervised hand is the interior beat, shown; the number
lands bare, once, like numbers should.

## 4. Wardrobe failure

**State:** Dana · stage 7 · proud, likes an audience · getting dressed for
a party.

**Weak:**
> Dana tries to button her favorite jeans but she has become too fat for
> them. The button pops off dramatically and flies across the room. "Oops!"
> she giggles. "Guess I'm just too huge now!" She is not ashamed at all —
> in fact, she loves being so big and fat.

**Diagnosis:** "she has become / she is not ashamed / she loves" — three
narrated verdicts and zero staging; the pop is "dramatic" by assertion;
the dialogue says the subtext out loud and kills it.

**Strong:**
> She saves the jeans for last, the way you save the good part. Lying
> back across the bed, breath held theatrical, tongue between her teeth —
> the zipper concedes a half inch, stalls, and the button and buttonhole
> regard each other across open country. Dana looks at the gap. Then at
> you. Then, holding your eyes, she inhales on purpose. The tok of the
> button hitting the closet door is small; the grin that follows it is
> not. "Get the camera," she says, still lying there, belly rising warm
> and unbuttoned through the ruin of the fly. "This deserves a eulogy."

**Why it works:** pride is choreography — she stages her own failure and
recruits a witness; the failure is physical and specific (the half inch,
the stall, the tok); the one flourish ("open country") is spent where the
tension peaks and nowhere else.

## 5. Mirror / interior

**State:** Elise · stage 3 · secret stance · alone, late.

**Weak:**
> Elise looks at herself in the mirror and secretly likes what she sees.
> Her body is getting softer and curvier every week. She would never
> admit it to anyone, but she hopes she keeps gaining weight. She smiles
> at her reflection and goes to bed.

**Diagnosis:** "secretly likes / would never admit / hopes" — the whole
secret handed over in narration, so there's no secret left; no touch, no
temperature, no risk of being caught; the scene has no verbs a body could
perform.

**Strong:**
> The bathroom door has a lock and she uses it, which is new. Elise lifts
> the hem of her sleep shirt the careful way you'd lift a bandage — then
> just holds it there, looking. The overhead light is honest. Her hand
> comes up and presses, lightly, into the new give at her waist, and the
> flesh accepts the fingertips the way a fresh loaf does, and she watches
> herself feel it. In the morning she'll take the stairs. She tells the
> mirror this. The mirror, which has heard it before, watches her fingers
> sink in one more time before the shirt comes down.

**Why it works:** secrecy is staged (the lock, the held hem) instead of
announced; the want lives in a repeated touch the narration never labels;
the denial line is undercut by the same frame, gently, without the
narrator taking sides.

## 6. Public / being seen

**State:** Coral · stage 9 · owned, unbothered · campus dining hall, noon.

**Weak:**
> Everyone stares at Coral as she walks through the dining hall because
> she is so enormous now. She takes up a lot of space between the tables.
> But Coral doesn't care what anyone thinks. She fills her tray with a
> huge amount of food and sits down proudly at her usual spot.

**Diagnosis:** "everyone stares / doesn't care / proudly" — audience,
psychology, and pride all asserted, none rendered; the room has no
geometry; the tray has no contents.

**Strong:**
> The dining hall renegotiates around Coral the way traffic does around
> a parade. She comes through the aisle at her own speed — there is only
> her own speed — hips reading the chair-backs on both sides like braille,
> and conversation dips behind her in a slow wake and recovers. Someone at
> the pasta line steps back a full step and pretends it was to check his
> phone. Coral thanks him with a nod that says she saw both things. Her
> usual table has, since spring, one chair fewer and one bench more; she
> takes the bench the way other people take a throne, sets down a tray
> built in load-bearing layers, and starts, unhurried, with the warm
> bread.

**Why it works:** the audience reacts in specifics (the wake, the phone
excuse) and she registers it with power instead of armor; the environment
has adapted to her (the bench) — worldbuilding as compliment; scale reads
through navigation, never through a stat.

## 7. Immobility / scale

**State:** Vess · ceiling rung of the ladder · settled sovereignty · her
room, morning; her partner attends.

**Weak:**
> Vess is now so gigantic that she cannot move at all anymore. She fills
> up most of the room with her massive, immense, colossal body. You have
> to do everything for her because she is completely helpless. She doesn't
> mind though, and asks for her breakfast.

**Diagnosis:** "cannot / helpless / everything for her" frames absence
instead of presence; three size adjectives stacked where one image should
be; her agency is an afterthought ("doesn't mind though").

**Strong:**
> Morning reaches Vess in installments — the window warms one flank long
> before the other, and she hums at the sunlight like it's staff arriving
> late. The room has been hers for a year now the way a hull belongs to a
> ship: the bed built where she is, the shelves migrated to the reach of
> her arm, the good cups living on the near table because that is where
> the good cups are needed. You bring the tray to the shore of her. "The
> jam first," she says, and tips her chin so you can tuck the napkin under
> it, and the great warm weight of her shifts a degree toward you —
> unhurried, tectonic, entirely on purpose — which is how she pulls you,
> these days, into the orbit where kisses happen.

**Why it works:** the verbs are hers (hums, says, tips, pulls) — stillness
with command; scale is architecture and warmth ("the shore of her"), never
deficit; the devotion runs both directions and ends in her power move.

## 8. Dialogue (feeder dominance as care; two voices)

**State:** Nadia (feedee, eager, chef) and Rowan (feeder, calm) · stage 6 ·
kitchen, midnight.

**Weak:**
> "Are you still hungry, my dear?" Rowan asked caringly.
> "Yes! I want to eat so much more! Feed me!" Nadia exclaimed excitedly.
> "You are getting so wonderfully fat," Rowan said dominantly.
> "I know! I love gaining weight for you!" she replied happily.

**Diagnosis:** adverb attribution doing the acting ("caringly",
"dominantly"); both voices identical — swap the name tags and nothing
breaks; the kink stated in its own vocabulary instead of the characters'.

**Strong:**
> Rowan sets the pan down to soak and doesn't turn around. "There's a
> plate in the oven with your name on it."
>
> "It's midnight." Nadia is already off the stool; the floorboard under
> it sighs with relief and gives her away.
>
> "It's bread pudding. Midnight is when it's correct." He turns then,
> towel over one shoulder, and watches her open the oven like it might be
> a trick. "Sit. Both spoons."
>
> "Both — Rowan, it's a *family size* —"
>
> "And you're a professional." He puts the dish in front of her, and the
> steam climbs her face, and whatever she'd loaded next comes apart. He
> pulls his own chair around to her side of the table, close enough that
> his knee rests against the soft new width of her thigh. "There's no
> hurry," he says, in the voice he'd use to lock a door. "We have until
> you're done."

**Why it works:** strip the tags and the voices still sort — she runs hot
and interrupted, he speaks in verdicts; the dominance is logistics and
patience ("both spoons," the moved chair), never announced; one physical
detail (knee against thigh) carries what four adverbs failed to.

## 9. Addendum — decomposing a strong passage into engine form

Exemplar 1's strong portrait, as the skeleton it would ship as:

```
{port.furnitureCue} {port.habitShift} {port.garmentPeace} {port.reachBeat}
```

| Slot | Shape | Keying | The exemplar's line |
|---|---|---|---|
| `port.furnitureCue` | SENT | stage band × locale, asserts `seat.state` | "The desk chair announces her…" |
| `port.habitShift` | SENT | corruption/stance (this is the psych beat) | "…has stopped waiting for that sound…" |
| `port.garmentPeace` | SENT | fitTop × stance, forbids `garment.top: adjusted` | "Her shirt rides where it used to fall, and she leaves it…" |
| `port.reachBeat` | SENT | stageMin 4, optional (`""` at low stages) | "When she reaches for her mug…" |

Notes that generalize: the name lives in ONE slot (`habitShift`); the
chair line asserts a fact later furniture lines must respect; the shirt
beat is the stance carrier — at `opposed` its pool holds the tugging-down
variants, and the two never pool together. Write the strong passage
first, then cut it at the joints; passages decomposed before they exist
come out sounding like slots.

## 10. The pattern under all eight

Weak versions share one root failure: they **assert conclusions** — big,
comfortable, proud, helpless, dominant. Strong versions stage evidence and
let the reader convict. Before shipping any passage, find every adjective
of size and every named emotion, and ask what a witness could have seen,
heard, or felt instead. That substitution is 80% of this genre's craft;
the other 20% is knowing which single flourish to keep.
